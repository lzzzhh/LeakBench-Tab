\begin{abstract}
Leakage repair claims often conflate four objects: whether information is
valid at prediction time, whether a blind policy can localize it, whether a
learner exploits it, and whether deletion restores a boundary-valid
evaluation. We introduce CDXR, a contract-grounded evaluation architecture
that separates construction validity (C), detectability (D), exploitability
(X), and repair (R) through explicit information, policy-access, cost, and
falsification interfaces. LeakBench-Tab instantiates CDXR with \CoreCellCount{}
paired measurement cells and a \FullBOneCellCount{}-cell Full-B1 governance
execution over 5,500 keys, five policies, two cost contracts, three budgets,
and 20 matched-random seeds. Under the primary semantic-group 20\% contract,
blind mutual-information removal (P3) improves directional repair over random
removal by \PrimaryDirection{} (95\% task-cluster CI
[\PrimaryDirectionLow{},\PrimaryDirectionHigh{}]) and complete leakage-group
recall by \PrimaryGroupRecall{}
([\PrimaryGroupRecallLow{},\PrimaryGroupRecallHigh{}]). Yet it also increases
overcorrection by \PrimaryOvercorrection{}
([\PrimaryOvercorrectionLow{},\PrimaryOvercorrectionHigh{}]), while its legacy
score-distance interval crosses zero (\PrimarySdr{},
[\PrimarySdrLow{},\PrimarySdrHigh{}]). Three additional blind policies exhibit
the same localization--overcorrection tension, so the joint gate classifies all
four as tradeoffs rather than successful repairs. A separately frozen
LR/RF/LightGBM amendment finds no reliable learner interaction for the narrower
encoded-column P3 score contrast, but all learners fail the overcorrection
gate. CDXR thus contributes more than a benchmark ranking: it specifies when a
repair claim is admissible and can falsify apparently positive score recovery
when semantic and intervention outcomes disagree.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Tabular prediction pipelines routinely inherit fields whose values are only
available after the prediction event, are derived from the target, or summarize
future entities and outcomes. Such contamination can invalidate evaluation and
distort model comparison even when the learning algorithm and held-out split
are otherwise conventional \cite{kaufman2011leakage,kapoor2023leakage}. The
practical response is often to remove suspicious fields, but this response
combines three different questions: whether a field is semantically valid at
prediction time, whether an oracle-blind statistic can locate it, and whether a
learner exploits it enough to change measured performance.

Existing leakage studies establish the importance of prediction boundaries,
scientific validity, and domain-specific label leakage
\cite{davis2024framework,rosenblatt2024connectome}. Detection work also spans
code analysis and empirical contamination audits
\cite{subotic2022static,magar2022contamination}. These perspectives do not by
themselves determine whether a deployable, budget-limited removal policy will
repair an affected tabular pipeline. A detector can rank target proxies highly
yet fail on future aggregation or entity structure. Conversely, a semantically
invalid field can be hard to rank but still inflate a learner's score.

This gap matters because leakage response is an intervention, not merely a
diagnosis. Removing a field changes the representation on which the learner is
fitted and may discard legitimate predictive information along with the
contamination. Comparing a removal policy only with the unmodified table
confounds two effects: locating invalid information and reducing model
capacity. Comparing it only with an oracle mask instead asks whether the policy
is perfect, not whether its ranking is useful under realistic constraints. A
matched random policy supplies the missing negative control: it estimates how
much movement toward a strict reference is obtained merely by deleting the
same number of encoded fields.

A credible benchmark for this question must preserve several separations at
once. Semantic invalidity must be declared before examining feature scores;
diagnostics must be fitted without the contamination mask; strict, permissive,
and governed learners must share task content and test rows; and uncertainty
must respect the task hierarchy rather than treating repeated mechanisms,
strengths, seeds, or models as independent datasets. These requirements turn a
seemingly simple feature-ranking comparison into a protocol-design problem.

We address this protocol-design problem with \emph{CDXR}, an evaluation
architecture rather than a new selector. CDXR makes four interfaces explicit.
An information contract freezes prediction-time validity; a policy contract
enumerates exactly which training data and metadata a selector may access; a
cost contract maps semantic groups to charged intervention units; and an
evaluation contract computes an oracle-isolated repair vector after selection.
The oracle mask is therefore neither a training label nor hidden tuning input.
It is an evaluation object that can falsify a repair claim. Lineage-aware,
time-aware, and group-aware policies can be attached to the same interface by
declaring additional policy-visible metadata without changing the evaluator.

LeakBench-Tab is the controlled implementation of this architecture. It
crosses eleven audited mechanisms with tasks, strengths, injection seeds, and
learners for C/D/X measurement, then executes Full-B1 over four learned
policies, 20 matched-random realizations, two cost units, and three budgets for
R. Our analysis asks four questions. \textbf{RQ1} characterizes how D and X
vary across mechanisms. \textbf{RQ2} asks whether score recovery is accompanied
by directional and semantic repair rather than overcorrection. \textbf{RQ3}
tests policy and cost-contract sensitivity after integrating random-policy
variance. \textbf{RQ4} audits whether the narrower P3 score contrast interacts
with the downstream learner.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The paper makes four bounded contributions. First, it defines CDXR as an
executable architecture with typed inputs, policy-visible information,
cost maps, output vectors, and an explicit unsupported-claim gate. Second, it
provides a frozen factorial registry that varies task archetype, contamination
mechanism, strength, seed, and learner while preserving paired strict and full
evaluation. Third, Full-B1 evaluates four blind policies against 20-seed
matched random removal under semantic-group and encoded-column cost, producing
\FullBOneCellCount{} validated downstream cells. Fourth, the architecture
changes the scientific conclusion: learned policies improve localization and
directional movement, but their increased overcorrection prevents a general
repair claim. The artifact binds every paper-facing estimate to canonical
ledgers, protocol manifests, analysis code, and content hashes.

\section{CDXR Evaluation Architecture}

\subsection{Prediction Boundary and the C/D/X Axes}

Let $q=(t,m,a,s)$ index a controlled cell by task $t$, contamination
mechanism $m$, strength $a$, and injection seed $s$. Its encoded feature
universe is $\mathcal{F}_q$, and the construction contract identifies the
contaminated subset $\mathcal{L}_q\subseteq\mathcal{F}_q$. The strict and
permissive views are therefore
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{strict}}_q=\mathcal{F}_q\setminus\mathcal{L}_q,
 \qquad
 \mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{full}}_q=\mathcal{F}_q.
 \label{eq:feature-views}
\end{equation}
Each task declares an information boundary at prediction time $t_p$. For
encoded field $j$, construction validity is
\begin{equation}
  C_{qj}=\mathbf{1}\!\left[j\in\mathcal{L}_q\right]
  =\mathbf{1}\!\left[j\text{ is unavailable or target-derived at }t_p\right].
  \label{eq:construction-validity}
\end{equation}
$C_{qj}$ is fixed by task semantics and mechanism construction. It is not inferred
from association, attribution, or downstream performance.

An oracle-blind diagnostic assigns score $s_{qj}$ without reading $C_{qj}$ or the
test rows. We measure localization with average precision and normalize it by
the contaminated field prevalence $\pi$:
\begin{equation}
  \pi_q=\frac{|\mathcal{L}_q|}{|\mathcal{F}_q|},
  \qquad
  D_q = \frac{\mathrm{AP}_q-\pi_q}{1-\pi_q}.
  \label{eq:detectability}
\end{equation}
The primary diagnostic is mutual information computed on training rows with a
fixed random state. D is conditional on the diagnostic and representation.

For learner $\ell$, exploitability is the paired AUROC difference
\begin{equation}
  X_{q\ell} = A^{\mathrm{full}}_{q\ell}
      - A^{\mathrm{strict}}_{q\ell},
  \label{eq:exploitability}
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ indexes the downstream learner and $A$ denotes test AUROC.
The strict view removes construction-labeled contaminated fields; the
permissive view retains them. Positive X measures performance inflation under
this protocol. An interval containing zero is reported as an imprecise or
unsupported directional effect, not as evidence of equivalence.

CDXR completes these measurements with repair outcome $R$. Let an information
contract be
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal{I}_q=(t_p,\mathcal{F}_q,\mathcal{G}_q,
 \mathcal{L}_q,\rho_q),
 \label{eq:information-contract}
\end{equation}
where $t_p$ is the prediction event, $\mathcal{G}_q$ partitions encoded
columns into semantic groups, and $\rho_q$ records availability or lineage
evidence. A policy contract declares an access set
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal{A}_r\subseteq
 \{X^{\rm train},y^{\rm train},\mathcal{G},t,\mathrm{entity},\rho\}
 \label{eq:access-set}
\end{equation}
and must satisfy
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal{L}_q\notin\mathcal{A}_r
 \quad\text{for every non-oracle policy }r.
 \label{eq:oracle-isolation}
\end{equation}
Thus a group-aware selector may read $\mathcal{G}_q$, and a time-aware selector
may read training timestamps, but neither may read the held-back invalidity
mask.

\subsection{Governance Objective}

For policy $r$, cost contract $c$, and budget $b$, let
$\mathcal{R}_{q,r,c,b}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_q$ be its selected removal set and
$\kappa_{q,c}(R)$ its charged cost. Encoded-column cost counts columns; semantic
cost counts every touched group once:
\begin{equation}
 \kappa_{q,\mathrm{col}}(R)=|R|,\qquad
 \kappa_{q,\mathrm{grp}}(R)=
 |\{g\in\mathcal{G}_q:g\cap R\neq\varnothing\}|.
 \label{eq:cost-map}
\end{equation}
The governed view and matched-cost constraint are
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{gov}}_{q,r,c,b}
 =\mathcal{F}_q\setminus\mathcal{R}_{q,r,c,b},
 \qquad \kappa_{q,c}(\mathcal{R}_{q,r,c,b})=b.
 \label{eq:governed-view}
\end{equation}
Let $A_s$, $A_f$, and $A_g$ denote strict, full, and governed test AUROC for a
fixed $q$ and learner. We define strict distance reduction (SDR) as
\begin{equation}
 \mathrm{SDR}_{q,r,k}
 = |A_{f,q}-A_{s,q}|-|A_{g,q,r,k}-A_{s,q}|.
 \label{eq:sdr}
\end{equation}
Positive SDR means governance moves the evaluated learner closer to the strict
reference. This symmetric distance avoids treating negative residual harm as
automatic success. We also report contaminated field recall and legitimate
retention:
\begin{align}
 \mathrm{Rec}_{q,r,k}
 &=\frac{|\mathcal{R}_{q,r,k}\cap\mathcal{L}_q|}{|\mathcal{L}_q|},
 \label{eq:recall}\\
 \mathrm{Ret}_{q,r,k}
 &=1-\frac{|\mathcal{R}_{q,r,k}\setminus\mathcal{L}_q|}
 {|\mathcal{F}_q\setminus\mathcal{L}_q|}.
 \label{eq:retention}
\end{align}
These outcomes are evaluation quantities and can read $C$ after a policy has
selected fields; non-oracle policy selection cannot.

SDR alone cannot distinguish correct repair from crossing past the strict
reference. With $h_q=A_{f,q}-A_{s,q}$ and
$z_{q,r}=A_{g,q,r}-A_{s,q}$, define
\begin{align}
 \mathrm{Dir}_{q,r}&=|h_q|-\max\{\operatorname{sgn}(h_q)z_{q,r},0\},
 \label{eq:directional-repair}\\
 \mathrm{Over}_{q,r}&=\max\{-\operatorname{sgn}(h_q)z_{q,r},0\}.
 \label{eq:overcorrection}
\end{align}
For semantic groups $\mathcal{H}_q\subseteq\mathcal{G}_q$ labeled invalid,
complete-group recall is
\begin{equation}
 \mathrm{GRec}_{q,r}={1\over|\mathcal{H}_q|}
 \sum_{g\in\mathcal{H}_q}\mathbf{1}[g\subseteq\mathcal{R}_{q,r}].
 \label{eq:group-recall}
\end{equation}
CDXR returns the repair vector
\begin{equation}
 R_{q,r,c,b}=(\mathrm{SDR},\mathrm{Dir},\mathrm{GRec},
 \mathrm{Rec},\mathrm{Ret},\mathrm{Over},\mathrm{Dist}_0).
 \label{eq:repair-vector}
\end{equation}
A score-recovery claim is \emph{unsupported} when its required SDR interval
crosses zero. A semantic-repair claim is also unsupported when localization or
direction fails, or when the policy improves them only by reliably increasing
overcorrection or zero-opportunity distortion. This falsification rule is the
architectural difference between CDXR and a renamed metric taxonomy.

\subsection{Evaluation Conditions}

Four conditions distinguish the benchmark from ordinary feature selection.
\textit{Semantic precedence} requires $\mathcal{L}_q$ to be determined by the
prediction-time contract rather than by a score threshold. A feature can be
invalid with low marginal association, or valid with high association; neither
case is contradictory. \textit{Oracle isolation} requires the selection map
$\mathcal{R}_{q,r,k}=\phi_r(X_q^{\mathrm{train}},y_q^{\mathrm{train}},k)$
to exclude $\mathcal{L}_q$ for non-oracle policies. The mask enters only after
$\mathcal{R}_{q,r,k}$ is frozen, when recall and retention are evaluated.

\textit{Cost matching} holds $\kappa_{q,c}$ fixed within each policy
comparison. One-hot expansion makes a semantic field and an encoded column
different intervention units: the eight M09 source indicators cost eight under
$c=\mathrm{col}$ and one under $c=\mathrm{grp}$. Full-B1 executes both
contracts rather than retrospectively recomposing one from the other. The
semantic-group contract is primary for repair validity; encoded-column cost is
the representation sensitivity.

\textit{Paired reference evaluation} keeps the task, split, mechanism,
strength, injection seed, and learner seed fixed when strict, full, and
governed AUROC are compared. SDR is bounded by $[-1,1]$ because it is a
difference of two AUROC distances. Positive SDR indicates movement toward the
strict score, but not recovery of the strict feature set: a policy may reach a
similar AUROC after removing the wrong fields. Recall and retention are
reported alongside SDR precisely to reveal that ambiguity. Likewise, negative
SDR identifies movement away from the strict reference but does not by itself
distinguish residual contamination from over-removal of legitimate signal.

These conditions define the scope of a governance claim. A policy is useful in
this benchmark only when it improves the paired distance under equal removal
cost and its localization trade-off remains visible. The formulation does not
assert that the strict model is optimal for a business objective, only that it
is the evaluation reference consistent with the declared information
boundary.

\subsection{Composable Policies and Claim Admission}

A CDXR policy plug-in is the tuple
\begin{equation}
 \Pi_r=(\mathcal{A}_r,\sigma_r,\tau_r,\kappa_c),
 \label{eq:policy-plugin}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_r$ maps accessible training information to column or group
scores, $\tau_r$ is a deterministic tie and truncation rule, and $\kappa_c$ is
the charged cost map. The selector emits only a selection hash and the removed
group/column identifiers. A separate evaluator joins that immutable output to
$\mathcal{L}_q$ and test predictions. This interface has two practical
consequences. First, a new policy can consume lineage or temporal metadata by
expanding $\mathcal{A}_r$ without modifying the oracle evaluator. Second, two
policies cannot be compared unless their $\kappa_c$, budget, downstream
learner, split, and task key agree.

Let $L(u)$ and $U(u)$ denote the lower and upper limits of a task-cluster
interval for repair-coordinate contrast $u$. The frozen semantic-corroboration
gate is
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
 \mathsf{Sem}(r)=\mathbf{1}[&L(\Delta\mathrm{Dir})>0\ \land
 L(\Delta\mathrm{GRec})>0\\
 &\land\ \overline{\Delta\mathrm{Over}}\leq0
 \land\ \overline{\Delta\mathrm{Ret}}\geq0
 \land\ \overline{\Delta\mathrm{Dist}_0}\leq0].
\end{split}
 \label{eq:semantic-gate}
\end{equation}
If localization is confirmed but an adverse coordinate is also confirmed, the
status is \textsc{tradeoff}. Localization without downstream corroboration is
\textsc{localization-only}; positive SDR alone is
\textsc{score-recovery-only}; otherwise the result is negative or not
evaluable. The precedence is fixed before effect inspection.

CDXR rejects a repair claim before statistics when its prediction event is
undeclared, a non-oracle selector can read $\mathcal{L}_q$, intervention costs
are unmatched, or strict/full/governed evaluations do not share the same task
key. After statistics, it rejects wording stronger than the claim-gate status.
This separation between protocol admission and empirical corroboration makes
the architecture usable as both a benchmark harness and a reviewer-facing
validity check.

\section{Benchmark and Experimental Design}

\subsection{Controlled Registry}

The controlled registry contains 20 deterministic panel tasks spanning five
data-generating archetypes. Each task has a chronological 60/20/20 split and
separates generator seeds from injection seeds. Eleven mechanisms cover direct
target copies (M01), noisy proxies (M02), nonlinear transforms (M03),
post-outcome aggregates (M04), temporal look-ahead (M05), redundant clusters
(M06), sparse subgroup leakage (M07), entity leakage (M08), source leakage
(M09), mixed legitimate and contaminated components (M10), and graph-mediated
leakage (M11). Categories are assigned by construction: M01, M02, M06, and M10
are simple; M03, M07, and M11 are boundary mechanisms; M04, M05, M08, and M09
are structured. Categories are not revised after observing results.

The five base archetypes are linear, interaction, nonlinear, sparse, and
drifting binary prediction tasks. Each contains entity and time structure and
between 12 and 24 original numeric fields. Clean signal combines row-level
covariates with entity effects and a periodic time component; the archetype
then changes the functional form rather than the evaluation contract. Four
tasks instantiate each archetype. Task generator seeds are deterministic
functions of the panel identifier, so changing an injection or learner seed
cannot regenerate the clean table. All tasks are ordered by timestamp before
the 60/20/20 split, and injection is verified not to alter the frozen row
indices.

This panel is designed for controlled variation rather than realism by volume.
It supplies repeated task structure in which semantic contracts and injected
features are exactly auditable. The five natural cases introduced below serve
a different purpose: they test whether boundary-specific contamination remains
visible in selected real tables, but they do not replace the controlled panel
as the inferential unit.

\begin{sloppypar}
The core matrix crosses 20 tasks, 11 mechanisms, five strengths, five
injection seeds, and five learners, yielding \CoreCellCount{} paired cells.
Logistic regression is the linear baseline. The remaining learners are random
forest \cite{breiman2001random}, CatBoost \cite{prokhorenkova2018catboost},
LightGBM \cite{ke2017lightgbm}, and TabM \cite{gorishniy2025tabm}.
Preprocessing and diagnostic fitting use training data only. The design spans
linear, bagged-tree, boosted-tree, and neural tabular families without treating
family differences as causal capacity effects.
\end{sloppypar}

\subsection{Mechanism Families and Strength Semantics}

The registry varies how invalid information becomes available to a learner.
The \emph{simple} family contains fields whose encoded columns directly expose
or redundantly repeat target information. M01 copies the current label with a
strength-dependent flip rate; M02 adds noise to a centered target proxy; M06
creates a correlated cluster whose size increases across strength levels; and
M10 pairs one legitimate copy of an original field with one contaminated
target proxy. M10 is deliberately mixed: the strict view removes only its
contaminated component and retains the legitimate component, preventing the
oracle reference from benefiting merely by deleting an entire injected group.

The \emph{structured} family makes validity depend on relations not represented
by a single contemporaneous field. M04 aggregates labels from a strictly future
window; M05 introduces a configurable look-ahead offset; and M08 computes a
shrunken, strictly future label rate within entity. All three exclude the
current row's own label. M09 assigns one of eight source categories with
outcome-dependent source probabilities and represents the source as a complete
one-hot block. It does not use target-rate encoding, but the source identity is
deployment-invalid under the declared construction. Consequently, its
detectability and removal cost are representation-conditional.

The \emph{boundary} family stresses cases between those poles. M03 multiplies
a target-derived signal by the side of a threshold on a legitimate field,
hiding marginal association behind an interaction. M07 exposes a target proxy
only within a sampled subgroup, with subgroup prevalence varying by strength.
M11 distributes target-derived signal across a connected component whose size
also varies with strength. These mechanisms are not claimed to enumerate all
forms of leakage. They are controlled probes for directness, sparsity,
interaction, redundancy, time, entity, source, and group structure.

Strength labels S1--S5 correspond to values 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, and 1.0, but
the operational parameter is mechanism-specific. Strength scales signal for
direct proxies, changes subgroup coverage for M07, and changes group size for
M06 and M11. It therefore defines an ordered intervention within a mechanism,
not a common physical scale across mechanisms. Cross-mechanism comparisons
average the frozen five-level profile; they do not interpret S3 in one
mechanism as quantitatively equivalent to S3 in another.

\subsection{Diagnostics and Learner Protocol}

The primary diagnostic assigns each encoded field a univariate MI score using
training rows and a fixed random state. Localization is evaluated after scoring
with normalized average precision, which adjusts for the mechanism-specific
prevalence of contaminated encoded columns. Average precision is appropriate
for this imbalanced ranking problem because most fields are legitimate and the
relevant operating region is concentrated near the top of the ranking
\cite{saito2015precision}. We also record top-five contaminated-field recall as
an operational summary, but the normalized AP metric supplies D in the C/D/X
analysis.

A frozen sensitivity suite evaluates three alternatives: absolute Pearson
correlation on training rows, absolute coefficients from standardized logistic
regression, and validation-set permutation importance from a training-fitted
random forest. None receives the contamination mask before producing scores.
The four-method suite contains 22,000 successful diagnostic cells. Because the
protocol did not freeze simultaneous paired method-comparison intervals, these
results diagnose method dependence but do not support an inferential ranking of
detectors. In particular, a retrospectively selected best method is an
optimistic labeled-benchmark summary, not a deployable selector.

The downstream panel fixes five named implementations. LR uses standardized
features and logistic regression; RF uses 250 trees; CatBoost and LightGBM use
validation-only early stopping; and TabM uses the official pinned package with
32 ensemble members, training-fitted standardization, deterministic execution,
and validation-loss checkpointing. Test labels are used only to compute final
AUROC. Missing packages or accelerators fail a cell rather than triggering a
fallback under another model identity. The completed canonical matrix contains
all 27,500 planned model cells, so no failed-cell exclusion enters the reported
means.

\subsection{Matched-Cost Governance Policies}

The governance experiment uses the same 5,500 combinations of task, mechanism,
strength, and seed. Full-B1 uses logistic regression downstream so policy and
cost effects are not confounded with a learner expansion. P0 retains all
fields, and P1 removes the construction-labeled mask; both are analysis-time
references. P2 samples uniformly under 20 frozen governance seeds. P3 ranks by
training-side mutual information, P4 by absolute point-biserial correlation,
P5 by the absolute coefficient of a standardized logistic model, and P6 by
three-fold cross-fitted RF permutation importance. P2--P6 receive only the
declared training view and opaque group membership. If $\omega\in\Omega$
denotes one random realization and $s_{q,r}(j)$ is the training-only score for
deterministic policy $r$, then
\begin{align}
 \mathcal{R}_{q,\mathrm{P2},c,b}^{(\omega)}
 &\sim \mathrm{Unif}\{R:\kappa_{q,c}(R)=b\},
 \label{eq:p2}\\
 \mathcal{R}_{q,r,c,b}
 &=\operatorname{Top}_{\kappa_{q,c}=b}
 \{s_{q,r}(j):j\in\mathcal{F}_q\},\quad r\in\{\mathrm{P3{:}P6}\}.
 \label{eq:learned-policy}
\end{align}
For nominal fractions $p\in\{5\%,10\%,20\%\}$ and cost-universe size
$d_{q,c}$, every policy uses
\begin{equation}
  b_{q,c}(p)=\max\!\left(1,\mathrm{round}(p d_{q,c})\right).
 \label{eq:budget}
\end{equation}
Thus the comparison satisfies the cellwise cost identity
\begin{equation}
 \kappa_{q,c}(\mathcal{R}_{q,\mathrm{P2}}^{(\omega)})
 =\kappa_{q,c}(\mathcal{R}_{q,r})=b_{q,c}(p).
 \label{eq:matched-cost}
\end{equation}
For every repair coordinate $u$, the paired estimand is
\begin{equation}
 \Delta^{(u)}_{q,r,c,p}=R^{(u)}_{q,r,c,p}
 -{1\over|\Omega|}\sum_{\omega\in\Omega}
 R^{(u,\omega)}_{q,\mathrm{P2},c,p}.
 \label{eq:full-b1-estimand}
\end{equation}
P2 variance is integrated inside each key before task resampling.

The clean runner loads frozen bundles, verifies bundle and split hashes, and
does not inject, split, or mutate data. Non-oracle selectors never receive the
contamination mask. Strict and full baselines are shared only within an
identical task, mechanism, strength, and seed key. All
\FullBOneGovernedCount{} governed rows and 11,000 baselines completed across
\GovernanceKeyCount{} keys, with zero failures or duplicate run identifiers.
A separately frozen encoded-column 20\% amendment repeats only P3 versus P2
for LR, RF, and LightGBM; it answers learner interaction for that narrow score
contrast and is not merged into Full-B1.

\subsection{Natural-Case Audit}

Five selected public or locally mirrored tables instantiate concrete prediction
boundaries: call completion for Bank Marketing, loan origination for Lending
Club, scheduled departure for U.S. flights, inspection completion for Chicago
food inspections, and complaint creation for NYC 311. For each task, the
strict view removes fields declared unavailable at that boundary and the
permissive view retains them. The harm audit crosses four CPU learner families
with three seeds, producing 60 paired cells. A separate LR governance
sensitivity uses the 20\% retained-feature budget, three training seeds, and 20
P2 governance seeds, producing 315 policy cells. The five cases are selected
rather than sampled, so both analyses remain descriptive fixed-case evidence.

Categorical preprocessing is fitted on training rows. Categories absent from
training map to a reserved unknown value, missing categories receive a separate
value, and date strings that had previously been globally encoded are dropped.
This policy prevents validation or test vocabularies from defining the
training representation. The case selection is intentionally heterogeneous in
table width and contamination count, but it is not random and does not define
a population of application datasets. One Lending Club source remains a local
mirror whose redistribution license is unresolved; the paper reports its
boundary-specific result but excludes the raw file from the public artifact.

\subsection{Freeze and Amendment Discipline}

Confirmatory execution is separated from protocol development. The canonical
manifest binds task bundles, split hashes, configuration, runner code, model
identity, and source result tables. Pilot namespaces use disjoint task seeds.
Once a confirmatory protocol is frozen, a change requires an explicit amendment
and replacement of the affected cells rather than silent in-place repair.

The canonical table incorporates four documented corrections. M04, M05, and
M08 use strictly future definitions that satisfy their construction contracts.
M10's strict mask retains its legitimate injected field. MI uses a fixed random
state on training data for the affected diagnostic cells, and natural-case
preprocessing is training-fitted. Superseded rows are
retained for provenance but excluded from canonical claim generation. These
amendments narrow the evidence to the final protocol and prevent earlier,
semantically different outputs from being averaged into the paper results.

The RF/LightGBM learner study is disclosed as a reviewer-motivated post-run
amendment rather than presented as prespecified Full-B1 scope. Its learner
factories, hyperparameters, seeds, and encoded-column 20\% estimand were frozen
before those outcomes were inspected. The amendment re-fits strict and full
baselines with the same factories instead of reusing earlier canonical fits,
preserves within-run pairing, and remains in a separate namespace. This timing
permits a bounded interaction sensitivity but not a claim that the three
learners or reported metrics were selected ex ante.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis and Claim Scope}

The controlled task is the independent unit. Core measurement intervals use a
20,000-draw hierarchical bootstrap that samples tasks and then injection seeds.
Mechanism tests use task-level sign flips with Holm correction over the eleven
mechanisms. The three category contrasts form a separate Holm family. The
prespecified simple-minus-structured contrast receives the core binary support
decision; individual mechanism profiles remain descriptive.

For clarity, let $\mathcal{V}$ denote the learner set, and let
$\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{si}}$ and $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{st}}$ denote the simple
and structured mechanism families. The category-level harm for task $t$ and
seed $s$ is
\begin{equation}
 \bar X_{t,s,c}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}||\mathcal{M}_c||\mathcal{A}|}
 \sum_{\ell\in\mathcal{V}}\sum_{m\in\mathcal{M}_c}
 \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}}X_{(t,m,a,s),\ell},
 \label{eq:category-harm}
\end{equation}
so the prespecified contrast is
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\Gamma}_{\mathrm{si-st}}
 =\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}||\mathcal{S}|}
 \sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}}\sum_{s\in\mathcal{S}}
 \left(\bar X_{t,s,\mathrm{si}}-\bar X_{t,s,\mathrm{st}}\right).
 \label{eq:core-contrast}
\end{equation}
Writing the seed-averaged paired contrast as $e_t$, its exact two-sided
task-level sign-flip value is
\begin{equation}
 p_{\mathrm{flip}}=2^{-|\mathcal{T}|}
 \sum_{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\in\{-1,+1\}^{|\mathcal{T}|}}
 \mathbf{1}\!\left[
 \left|\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_t\epsilon_t e_t\right|
 \geq\left|\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_t e_t\right|\right].
 \label{eq:sign-flip}
\end{equation}

For core confidence intervals, each bootstrap draw samples 20 tasks with
replacement and then samples five injection seeds with replacement inside each
selected task. All strengths, mechanisms, and learners associated with a drawn
task--seed pair remain together. This preserves the pairing that defines
$X_{q\ell}$ and prevents the 27,500 model cells from acting as independent
replicates. The simple-minus-structured interval is the 2.5th and 97.5th
percentiles of 20,000 hierarchical draws. Its exact sign-flip test first
averages seeds within task, enumerates all $2^{20}$ task-sign assignments, and
uses the two-sided absolute mean. Holm adjustment is applied jointly to the
three declared family contrasts. The eleven mechanism tests form a different
Holm family; their profiles remain descriptive regardless of adjusted value.

For every repair coordinate $u$, governance differences are first averaged
within each of the 20 controlled tasks. With $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{A}$, and
$\mathcal{S}$ denoting mechanisms, strengths, and injection seeds, the
task-level effect for policy $r$, contract $c$, and budget $p$ is
\begin{equation}
 d^{(u)}_{t,r,c,p}={1\over|\mathcal{M}||\mathcal{A}||\mathcal{S}|}
 \sum_{m\in\mathcal{M}}\sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}}\sum_{s\in\mathcal{S}}
 \Delta^{(u)}_{q,r,c,p},
 \label{eq:task-effect}
\end{equation}
and the registry-level estimand is the equal-task average
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\Delta}^{(u)}_{r,c,p}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}
 \sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}}d^{(u)}_{t,r,c,p}.
 \label{eq:registry-effect}
\end{equation}
For a mechanism family $h$ with member set $\mathcal{M}_h$, replacing
$\mathcal{M}$ by $\mathcal{M}_h$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:task-effect} gives
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\Delta}^{(u)}_{r,c,p,h}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}
 \sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}}d^{(u)}_{t,r,c,p,h}.
 \label{eq:family-effect}
\end{equation}
We assess these estimands with a paired 5,000-draw cluster bootstrap over
tasks. For draw $b$, tasks are sampled with replacement and the complete task
effect is carried with each sampled index:
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\Delta}^{*(b,u)}_{r,c,p}
 =\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{T}|}
 d^{(u)}_{T_i^{*(b)},r,c,p},
 \quad T_i^{*(b)}\stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}\mathrm{Unif}(\mathcal{T}).
 \label{eq:cluster-bootstrap}
\end{equation}
We report the observed mean task difference, percentile interval, and the
fraction of bootstrap draws above zero:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
 \mathrm{CI}_{0.95}
 &=\left[Q_{0.025}(\widehat{\Delta}^{*(u)}_{r,c,p}),
          Q_{0.975}(\widehat{\Delta}^{*(u)}_{r,c,p})\right],\\
 \widehat{P}_{\Delta^{(u)}>0}
 &=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\mathbf{1}
   [\widehat{\Delta}^{*(b,u)}_{r,c,p}>0].
\end{split}
 \label{eq:bootstrap-summary}
\end{equation}
The latter is a bootstrap superiority probability, not a $p$-value. Because
tasks form a designed registry rather than
a sample from a defined task population, all intervals quantify stability to
registry reweighting. They do not support population inference to arbitrary
tabular datasets.

\section{Measurement Results}

\subsection{Simple Contamination Produces More Harm on Average}

The prespecified simple-minus-structured contrast in paired AUROC harm was
\CoreContrast{} (95\% hierarchical bootstrap CI [\CoreContrastLow{},
\CoreContrastHigh{}]; Holm-adjusted exact task-level sign-flip
$p=\CoreContrastP{}$). The direction was positive across all five core learner
families. This category average does not imply that every structured mechanism
is harmless: M09 is a structured counterexample with mean harm 0.235.

The learner-family consistency is descriptive rather than a second support
decision. LR, bagged trees, boosted trees, and TabM differ in their ability to
represent interactions and partitions, but each yielded a positive
simple-minus-structured average. This observation reduces the likelihood that
the pooled direction is produced by one learner family alone. It does not show
that learner capacity causes the category contrast, because hyperparameters,
regularization, and representation are fixed components of the named model
implementations rather than randomized treatments.

\subsection{Detectability and Exploitability Form Crossed Profiles}

Mechanism profiles show why a single leakage severity score is insufficient.
M03 combines low primary-MI detectability (D=0.079) with substantial harm

The diagnostic sensitivity suite makes the conditioning on D concrete. Across

normalized AP ranges from 0.035 under absolute correlation to 0.602 under RF

curve. The benchmark can identify mechanisms for which a diagnostic and a

\subsection{Natural Cases Broaden Context but Not the Sampling Frame}

Five selected real-data case studies provide boundary-specific consistency
checks (Table~\ref{tab:natural}): UCI Bank Marketing \cite{uciBankMarketing},
a local Lending Club mirror, U.S. flight delays \cite{btsOnTime}, Chicago food
inspections \cite{chicagoFoodInspections}, and NYC 311 \cite{nyc311}. All have
positive mean paired harm, but their size, feature representation, and
contamination labels differ. The mean over these five selected tasks is
\NaturalMeanHarm{}. We do not use these cases for population inference.

The five case means span a wide range rather than forming a homogeneous
replication set. Flight and inspection boundaries show large observed harm,
whereas the NYC 311 boundary is close to the strict reference. This spread is
substantively useful: it shows that declaring a field unavailable does not
determine the magnitude of model exploitation. However, the tasks differ in
sample construction, table width, encoded representation, unavailable-field
count, and application semantics. Averaging them provides a compact audit
summary, not a transport estimate. The fact that all five observed means are
positive is reported as a fixed-case description and is not promoted to a
population claim.

The matched-cost governance sensitivity is also heterogeneous. The descriptive
mean P3-minus-mean-P2 SDR across the five cases is +0.187 (case-bootstrap
interval [+0.025,+0.323]), but the five cases do not form a population sample.
Bank Marketing (+0.102), Lending Club (+0.266), BTS Flights (+0.348), and
Chicago Food (+0.326) favor P3, whereas NYC311 favors P2 (-0.108). The exact
two-sided sign-flip value over the five fixed case effects is 0.1875. We
therefore classify natural governance as mixed descriptive evidence rather
than external confirmation. NYC311 also has only +0.013 initial harm. Its
negative repair contrast is therefore consistent with a low-opportunity case
in which deleting highly associated legitimate fields can cost more than the
small removable distortion; it is not evidence for a general natural-data
failure mode.

\subsection{Budget Traces a Localization--Overcorrection Frontier}

P3's semantic-group contrast changes systematically with budget. At 5\%, SDR
is +0.115 (CI [+0.098,+0.130]), directional repair is +0.120
([+0.106,+0.133]), and overcorrection is +0.005
([+0.001,+0.009]). At 10\%, the corresponding values are +0.085, +0.109, and
+0.024; at 20\%, they become +0.043, +0.095, and +0.052. Complete-group recall
remains large at all three fractions (+0.539, +0.537, and +0.483 relative to
P2). Increasing budget therefore does not monotonically improve repair. It
spends additional intervention capacity after many invalid groups are already
localized, increasing the probability of crossing the strict reference.

This pattern also explains why we do not choose 5\% retrospectively as the
``winning'' budget. The 5\% point has the strongest SDR and smallest
overcorrection, but the zero-tolerance semantic gate still detects a reliable
adverse coordinate, and the budget was one member of a frozen three-point
design. A deployment could prefer that point under a declared nonzero
overcorrection tolerance. CDXR requires that tolerance to be specified as part
of the utility or claim gate before selecting a result.

Mechanism decomposition shows that the frontier is not a family slogan. At
20\%, P3 has positive directional effects for M01, M02, M06, M09, M10, and
M11, but negative directional effects for M03--M05 and M08. M09 remains a
structured counterexample (+0.175 direction; +0.107 SDR), while M04, M05, and
M08 have negative SDR contrasts. Hence ``structured leakage fails'' is too
coarse: construction category, repair opportunity, marginal localization, and
group representation jointly determine the observed intervention.

Pairwise policy comparisons are likewise metric-scoped. For semantic-group
20\% SDR, P3 exceeds P4 by +0.030 (CI [+0.017,+0.045]) and P5 by +0.023
([+0.011,+0.038]), but its contrast with P6 crosses zero (+0.011,
[-0.003,+0.026]). Because P6 also has the largest full-group recall and positive
overcorrection, these rows define alternatives on a frontier rather than a
total order.

\subsection{Random Variance and Cost Are Part of the Estimand}

Random deletion is not a single convenient realization. Full-B1 evaluates 20
P2 seeds for every key, budget, and cost contract, then averages them before
forming each paired policy contrast. Thus uncertainty over which fields a
random policy happens to remove is integrated inside the estimand rather than
mistaken for between-task uncertainty.

The cost contract changes semantic behavior more than score behavior. At 20\%,
semantic-group minus encoded-column full-group recall is +0.144 for P3
(CI [+0.138,+0.150]), whereas the corresponding legacy-SDR change is -0.001
([-0.010,+0.007]). Semantic grouping prevents partial one-hot block removals
and improves the construct alignment of localization, but it does not create a
stronger score-recovery claim. This is precisely why $\kappa$ belongs in the
claim statement rather than in implementation footnotes.

\subsection{Archetype and Learner Boundaries}

The earlier sparse-regime failure is not reproduced under the formal primary
contract. P3's sparse legacy-SDR contrast is +0.016 (CI [-0.070,+0.067]), its
directional contrast is +0.077 ([+0.047,+0.106]), and overcorrection is +0.061

it is another repair tradeoff. Interaction tasks show the largest positive SDR
contrast (+0.089), but each archetype contains only four designed tasks, so
these rows are sensitivity analyses rather than independent environment-level
inference.

The separately frozen learner amendment is narrower than Full-B1. At
encoded-column 20\%, the legacy P3-minus-P2 SDR contrasts are +0.043 for LR,
+0.055 for RF, and +0.056 for LightGBM; direct interaction intervals cross
zero. We therefore report that no reliable learner interaction was detected
for this one score coordinate. We do not claim learner invariance: directional
repair exhibits a small LR disadvantage, and all three learners fail the
overcorrection gate. Cross-learner consistency of score recovery cannot rescue
a failed semantic-repair claim.

\subsection{What CDXR Changes}

Without CDXR, the encoded-cost P3 SDR interval could be summarized as a
positive governance result. The architecture rejects that shortcut twice.
First, semantic-group cost is the unit aligned with the M09 construction.
Second, the repair vector reveals that better localization and directional
movement coexist with reliable overcorrection. The defensible finding is not
that MI repairs leakage, nor that structured leakage defeats MI. It is that
blind association policies trace a reproducible localization--overcorrection
frontier whose acceptability depends on an explicit downstream utility.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Leakage semantics.}
Classical leakage formulations distinguish information that is available when
a model is developed from information that will be available when its
prediction is consumed \cite{kaufman2011leakage}. This semantic perspective is
stronger than a correlation rule: an aggressively predictive field may be
legitimate, while a weak future-derived field remains invalid. Recent work
links leakage to reproducibility in ML-based science \cite{kapoor2023leakage}
and formalizes domain-specific label leakage in health care
\cite{davis2024framework}. Empirical studies in connectome
prediction further show that leakage can inflate reported performance under
otherwise familiar modeling pipelines \cite{rosenblatt2024connectome}.
LeakBench-Tab adopts the same boundary-first principle, but turns it into a
factorial measurement contract in which validity, localization, and
exploitation are separate variables.

\paragraph{Detection at code, data, and representation levels.}
Static program analysis targets leakage pathways visible in data-science code,
such as fitting preprocessing outside the training split
\cite{subotic2022static}. Contamination audits in language benchmarks instead
often ask whether evaluation content was memorized and subsequently exploited
\cite{magar2022contamination}. Feature-level screening occupies another level:
it ranks columns from observed training data but typically cannot determine
whether their information is temporally or operationally available. These
approaches are complementary because they observe different evidence. Code
analysis can reveal an invalid fit scope without ranking columns; a statistical
ranker can reveal a target proxy without knowing its lineage; and a semantic
contract can identify a future field even when its sample association is weak.
Our CDXR architecture makes these non-equivalences explicit rather than
combining them into a single detector score.

\paragraph{Tabular evaluation under controlled shifts.}
TableShift studies tabular prediction under distribution shifts, while broad
comparative benchmarks examine when boosted trees, bagged trees, linear models,
and neural architectures succeed \cite{gardner2023tableshift,
mcelfresh2023tabzilla,grinsztajn2022trees}. Those resources vary datasets,
domains, or learner families to characterize predictive performance.
LeakBench-Tab holds a task instance and split fixed while perturbing the
information boundary through auditable mechanisms. Learner diversity is used
to measure exploitability conditional on representation, not to declare a new
winner among logistic regression, random forests, boosting, and TabM
\cite{breiman2001random,prokhorenkova2018catboost,ke2017lightgbm,
gorishniy2025tabm}. This focus also explains why the natural cases are treated
as boundary audits rather than merged with the controlled panel.

\paragraph{Feature selection versus leakage governance.}
Mutual information, linear coefficients, correlation, and permutation
importance measure predictive relevance under different function classes.
None is intrinsically a semantic validity test. Conventional feature selection
usually optimizes predictive performance or sparsity; leakage governance asks
whether a constrained intervention moves an evaluation toward a boundary-valid
reference while preserving legitimate information. Our P2--P6 comparison
therefore matches declared cost, records complete-group localization and
legitimate retention, decomposes movement into direction and overcorrection,
and applies a falsification gate. The technical contribution is the reusable
evaluation interface and its empirical instantiation, not a new ranker.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What the Evaluation Changes}

Leakage audits often end after declaring suspicious fields or measuring an
inflated score. Our results show that an actionable evaluation needs at least
four linked but distinct objects: the semantic contamination label, a blind
localization score, downstream exploitability, and the effect of a removal
policy under explicit cost. Reporting only one object hides important cases.
Low D does not validate a field, high X does not reveal which field caused the
inflation, and high D does not guarantee that removal will preserve legitimate
utility.

Matched-cost evaluation is central to this conclusion. Each learned policy is
compared with 20 random selections of the same charged cost, under both encoded
and semantic units. Even that comparison is insufficient by itself: positive
SDR can arise after crossing the strict reference or deleting the wrong fields.
The direction, group-recall, retention, and overcorrection coordinates expose
these paths.

\subsection{Implications for Leakage Audits}

The findings suggest an audit sequence rather than an automatic deletion rule.
An analyst should first declare the prediction event and label each field by
availability or lineage. Blind scores can then prioritize manual review, but
their rankings should be evaluated against a held-back semantic mask when a
benchmark is available. Finally, any removal policy should be compared with an
equal-cost negative control and assessed on downstream behavior. This sequence
prevents a high association score from silently becoming a validity judgment.

Budget and policy choice are governance decisions, not benchmark winners. A
deployment process needs a domain-specific utility over missed contamination,
lost legitimate signal, calibration, and decision consequences. Full-B1
deliberately preserves the vector outcome so a future user can declare that
utility without rerunning selection or retroactively choosing a favorable
metric.

The cost comparison shows why representation must be part of the audit
contract. Grouping M09's indicators strongly improves full-group recall while
barely changing the aggregate SDR contrast. Richer policies can add lineage,
temporal, or entity metadata through $\mathcal{A}_r$ without changing the
evaluator. The hidden contamination label must remain an evaluation object.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations and Required Follow-Ups}

The observed family pattern can have several compatible explanations. Simple
mechanisms may be easier for MI to rank because their invalid signal is carried
by individual encoded columns. Structured mechanisms may require group or
conditional statistics, but they may also exhibit weaker exploitability in the
particular generators, leaving less inflation for any policy to repair.
Learner representation adds a third possibility, although the similar LR, RF,
and LightGBM contrasts do not reveal a detectable learner interaction in the
present panel. The current factorial design describes the joint outcome of
these factors; it does not identify their separate causal contributions.

The largest remaining ambiguity concerns policies that use lineage, temporal,
or entity metadata rather than blind univariate association. These policies
would test whether low-gap structured mechanisms become governable when the
intervention represents their construction semantics. Natural evaluation also
remains narrow: five fixed cases cannot establish a population-level transport
claim, and the negative NYC311 effect requires application-specific explanation
rather than averaging away.

\subsection{Limitations}

The controlled tasks are synthetic panel generators, not twenty independent
real-world datasets. Their bootstrap intervals describe registry stability,
not a task population. Full-B1 uses LR downstream. The separately frozen P3
amendment covers LR, RF, and LightGBM only at encoded-column 20\%; those named
implementations do not establish invariance across model families, policies,
cost contracts, or tuning choices. Interaction intervals crossing zero are
absence of detected difference, not equivalence.

P2 uses 20 frozen random selections for each key, budget, and cost contract, and the
primary estimand averages P2 within key before task-cluster resampling. This
integrates the declared random-policy variance but does not cover every possible
randomization scheme. Nominal budget labels can also map to the same integer
removal count on tasks with few fields,
so adjacent budget points are not fully distinct interventions for every task.

All four learned policies are association-based. None encodes lineage,
temporal availability, entity semantics, or source provenance. Their tradeoff
statuses should not be generalized to metadata-aware policies. Conversely,
positive localization must not be read as evidence that association determines
validity.

The five real-data audits are selected, boundary-specific case studies. One
local Lending Club mirror cannot be redistributed until its source and license
are verified. AUROC distance does not replace calibration, decision utility,
or operational monitoring. Runtime and resource overhead are also outside the
current frozen evidence and should be added before making systems-efficiency
claims.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{Construct validity.} SDR measures movement toward a frozen strict
reference, not the business value of a deployed decision. Its interpretation
depends on the correctness of each declared prediction boundary and mechanism
contract. Recall and retention use the contamination mask only after policy
selection and are therefore evaluation measures rather than deployable
observables. Complete-group recall and both cost contracts address one-hot
expansion, but the quality of the semantic grouping remains part of the
information contract.

\textbf{Internal validity.} The paired protocol holds splits, task content,
model seeds, and removal cost fixed within each comparison. Bundle, split, and
runner hashes reduce implementation drift, but they cannot rule out all
library-specific behavior. Hyperparameters are fixed named
implementations rather than optimized independently for every mechanism; this
improves comparability but may understate the response of a specially tuned
learner. Protocol amendments are provenance-bound and replace affected cells,
yet their necessity also illustrates how semantic bugs can survive ordinary
completion checks.

\textbf{External and statistical validity.} The designed registry deliberately
varies mechanism structure, but it does not define a sampling frame of tabular
applications. Mechanism-family intervals and bootstrap probabilities should be
read as stability summaries for this registry. The natural cases broaden the
contexts inspected but remain selected case studies; their governance result is
mixed and descriptive. Multiplicity correction protects the declared contrast
families, but descriptive mechanism profiles and diagnostic sensitivity rows
should not be mined as independent confirmatory discoveries.

\section{Reproducibility and Artifact Boundary}

The release binds the canonical 27,500-cell measurement table, the 803,000-cell
Full-B1 merge, validation receipt, repair claim state, bootstrap analysis, and
paper-facing tables by SHA-256. The Full-B1 suite reports 312 passing tests with
no failures. Superseded runners and per-shard execution cache are excluded from
claim generation; the canonical merge and deterministic summaries are tracked.

Reproduction is organized in three layers. The \emph{paper layer} regenerates
all numeric macros, tables, and figures from compact governed CSVs and the
Full-B1 claim tables, and records their hashes in a paper-artifact manifest.
The \emph{analysis layer}
rebuilds measurement and governance summaries from canonical cell ledgers,
including the task-level bootstrap objects. The \emph{execution layer} reruns
frozen task bundles through named model and policy runners after verifying
bundle, split, configuration, and code identities. A reader can therefore
inspect the paper without rerunning models, recompute claims without rebuilding
tasks, or execute the complete experiment when the required packages and
hardware are available.

Claim state is machine-readable and distinct from prose. Each central claim
records an estimand, evidence tier, allowed wording, prohibited wording, and
scope limitation. The paper generator does not infer support from arbitrary
tables; it consumes the frozen paper-facing assets. This separation is intended
to prevent a later figure edit, exploratory table, or superseded run from
silently changing a conclusion.

The public artifact and reproduction instructions are maintained at
\url{https://github.com/lzzzhh/LeakBench-Tab}. The paper-facing artifact does
not redistribute the local-only Lending Club mirror. All non-oracle policy
selectors can be audited without access to the contamination mask; the mask is
used only for evaluation after selection.

\section{Conclusion}

CDXR turns leakage repair from an association ranking into a falsifiable
evaluation contract. Its interfaces specify prediction-time information,
policy-visible metadata, intervention cost, oracle isolation, paired outcomes,
and the conditions under which a repair claim is unsupported. LeakBench-Tab
instantiates the architecture at measurement and governance scale.

The conclusion is narrower than the legacy SDR result. P3--P6 improve complete
group localization and directional movement over 20-seed matched random
removal, but all increase overcorrection; their status is tradeoff.
Cross-learner score consistency does not remove that conflict. Future
metadata-aware policies can use the same CDXR interface and should be accepted
only when they improve the joint repair vector rather than one convenient
coordinate.
